Why did I get this result?

When a submission is made to our Machine Learning model, the model activates and utilizes the Natural Language Tool-Kit (NLTK) to analyze each word. When performing this analysis, some general words (such as "I," "you," "the," "do," "it" etc.) are removed from the analysis (but not from the input). Each word is then applied to a "Bag of Words," which measures how many times that word appears in a positive or a negative context. The input is then applied to a Naive-Bayes Classifier, which classifies each word as positive or negative based on its bag of words values. Words with a higher count in the positive bag of words have a higher probability of being labeled as positive, and vice-versa.

However, before the model could be applied to our data, it had to be "trained" to recognize positive or negative data. This was done by manually labeling a portion of our data, which required us to read individual tweets and determine whether they were positive or negative, as well as what candidates the tweets were discussing or mentioning.

As the model ran over the dataset in it's entirety, it applied this same analysis and weighted the words it encountered automatically and categorized them as the model deemed appropriate for that weight value.

An interesting caveat to this approach is that words or phrases that we associate with a certain emotion don’t necessarily have to reflect that emotion in the model. As an example, if several tweets are using the word “sick,” as in “Bernie Sanders is sick!”, "sick" becomes a positively-associated word. The probability of a word being categorized one way or another ultimately comes down to our original training of the model using "positive" and "negative" to identify whole tweets.

As a summary, our model was trained by reading and labeling entire tweets, not by pre-loading words into the algorythm with pre-defined weight values. Additionally, the sentiment label of a given word is based on it's probability of being positive or negative, based on the number of times that word was used in either context.

For an in-depth explanation of how the Naive-Bayes Classifier works, check out this article on Medium.com.

How the Model Predicts Sentiment

Our Machine Learning model was trained to recognize positive or negative sentiment on a dataset of approximately 14,000 tweets regarding the 2020 Nevada Presidential Caucus. Each word in a tweet is analyzed by our model using the Natural Language Tool-Kit (NLTK), which assigns each word a positive or a negative weighting.

The Model Word Weight Table displays the words that our model recognized as bearing the greatest weighting, along with it's predicted sentiment. The Model Word Sentiment Value line graph above visualizes the change of the sentiment values for the same set of words.

Because our model was trained using tweets about the 2020 Nevada Presidential Caucus, it is important to note that our predictor functionality will be more accurate when given an input discussing the 2020 Presidential race. Inputs provided to the model that do not pertain to the 2020 Presidential Race or the 2020 Nevada Caucus will still be analyzed by the model, although the predictions based on a broader input cannot be guaranteed to have the same level of accuracy.
